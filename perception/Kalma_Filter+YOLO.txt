Kalman Filter + YOLO Tracking Experiment
Test 1: Slow Movement

Test:
Move face slowly in front of the camera.

Observation:

Detection (YOLO) and prediction (Kalman) points move almost together
Very little difference between them
Tracking appears smooth and stable

Conclusion:
When motion is smooth and predictable, the Kalman filter performs very well because its prediction closely matches the actual movement.

Test 2: Fast Movement

Test:
Move face quickly across the frame.

Observation:

Kalman prediction lags behind the detection
Green point follows but with slight delay
Takes a few frames to adjust

Conclusion:
Kalman filter assumes smooth motion (constant velocity).
Sudden changes break this assumption, causing temporary lag until the filter adapts.

Test 3: Temporary Occlusion (Cover Face)

Test:
Cover face or move out of frame briefly.

Observation:

YOLO detection disappears
Kalman prediction continues moving in the previous direction
After reappearance, tracking resumes

Conclusion:
Kalman filter can predict object position even without measurements.
This is useful for handling occlusions or missed detections.

Test 4: Detection Noise

Test:
Stay still and observe detection jitter.

Observation:

YOLO detection (red point) slightly jumps
Kalman output (green point) remains smoother

Conclusion:
Kalman filter reduces noise by combining prediction and measurement, producing a more stable estimate.

Final Understanding
YOLO provides noisy position measurements
Kalman filter predicts motion and corrects using measurements
Final output is smoother and more reliable than raw detection
